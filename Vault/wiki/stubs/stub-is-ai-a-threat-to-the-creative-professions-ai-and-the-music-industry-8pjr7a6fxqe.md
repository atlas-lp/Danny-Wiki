# Is AI a Threat to the Creative Professions? AI and the Music Industry

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

This is a fireside chat from the NYU Stern Fubon Center's Demystifying AI Speaker Series (November 2023) featuring Dr. Howie Singer, former SVP and Chief Strategic Technologist at Warner Music, and NYU Stern Professor Foster Provost. The conversation explores whether generative AI constitutes an existential threat to creative professionals, particularly in the music industry, by examining both the opportunities AI already provides (such as predictive analytics for artist discovery) and the fears around substitution of human creative labor. The discussion contextualizes AI disruption within the music industry's long history of technological transformation, from the CD era through streaming, and addresses parallels with Hollywood's AI-related labor disputes.

## Key Claims

- The rapid introduction of generative AI systems is widely perceived as an existential threat to the livelihood of creative professionals, as articulated by the Society of Composers and Lyricists
- The music industry has historically resisted new technologies—even the CD was initially opposed by most labels and had to be pitched to convince them of its value
- AI is already broadly used in the music industry for non-generative purposes, such as predictive models that analyze social media signals (Instagram growth, TikTok virality) to identify future artist success
- The core fear around generative AI in music is substitution: creating new voices, instrumentation, lyrics, and accompaniment based on existing creative works without compensating original artists
- Hollywood studios sought to scan actors' faces and create digital extras using AI/CGI, raising the same substitution and compensation issues that emerged in the actors' and writers' contract disputes
