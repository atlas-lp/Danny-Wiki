# Mentor Network and Influences

## Tom Fawcett: The Foundational Collaborator

Provost's longest and most formative intellectual partnership is with Tom Fawcett. Their collaboration dates to at least 1996 ("Combining Data Mining and Machine Learning for Effective User Profiling") and produced the KDD-1997 Best Paper Award winner "Analysis and Visualization of Classifier Performance: Comparison under Imprecise Class and Cost Distributions." From Fawcett, Provost absorbed a deep concern with **evaluation methodology** — the idea that how you measure a model matters as much as the model itself, especially under imprecise class and cost distributions. This partnership culminated in *Data Science for Business*, which became a defining text for the field. The personal closeness is evident: Provost references shared travel stories — debating rabbit recipes at a Provençal market, adventures in the outskirts of Marseille — indicating a relationship where ideas were developed dialogically over decades of sustained personal contact.

## Vasant Dhar: The Stern Colleague and Intellectual Foil

Provost's relationship with Vasant Dhar at NYU Stern spans decades. Provost was Dhar's student in 1993, taking his first computer course at Stern. By 2025, the roles had inverted: Provost hosted Dhar for a public conversation about *Thinking With Machines*. Dhar's influence surfaces in Provost's engagement with the boundary between logical/command-based computation and something closer to machine insight — a theme Provost probes through his work on explainability and generative AI.

## David Martens: Privacy and Counterfactual Explanation

Martens is a key collaborator on the line of research connecting machine learning transparency to civil rights. Their joint work on Facebook Likes inference and the "cloaking device" for privacy control gave Provost a concrete paradigm for **instance-level counterfactual explanations** — a signature commitment that distinguishes his explainability work from model-level approaches.

## Carlos Fernández-Loría: Causal Data Science

Fernández-Loría co-authored the JMLR 2022 paper on causal classification and the ICIS 2020 work on combining observational and experimental data. Through this collaboration, Provost formalized his long-standing intuition that **non-causal models are routinely misused for causal interventions** — an idea that connects his ad-tech experience (Dstillery, Integral Ad Science) to formal causal reasoning influenced by Judea Pearl's framework.

## Solon Barocas and Vasant Honavar: Data Science as Civil Rights

The white paper "Big Data, Data Science, and Civil Rights" with Barocas and Honavar positioned Provost at the intersection of machine learning and societal accountability, reinforcing his instinct to frame technical problems in terms of their human and political stakes.

## Sinan Aral: Social Influence and Elections

Provost's public conversations with Aral about *The Hype Machine* reveal a shared concern with **algorithmically driven social influence** and election interference — connecting to Provost's cross-domain bridge between political science and causal reasoning in data science.
