# Foster Provost Is Bigger Really Better Predictive Analytics With Fine Grained Be 1Jzmiaflh2C

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

Foster Provost presents a talk from Strata 2013 examining whether "bigger data" truly confers competitive advantage in predictive analytics. Using a detailed case study from a major bank, he compares traditional predictive modeling (using sociodemographic, psychographic, and geographic variables) against models incorporating fine-grained individual behavioral data. He demonstrates through learning curve analysis that sparse, fine-grained consumer behavior data can yield significant predictive improvements, but whether bigger data is better depends critically on the characteristics of the data and the modeling capability applied to it.

## Key Claims

- Concrete results supporting the competitive advantage of big data in predictive analytics are surprisingly few and far between
- A major bank's sophisticated predictive modeling team used 200-300 traditional variables (sociodemographic, psychographic, geographic, prior experience) but did not use fine-grained individual behavioral transaction data
- Fine-grained individual behaviors (not aggregated to the customer level) can better predict individual tastes, interests, and proclivities for different products
- Whether bigger data is better depends on the characteristics of the data — specifically, sparse fine-grained data with massive numbers of individual behaviors each containing a small amount of information shows distinctive learning curve behavior
- Data should be treated as an asset with consideration of return on investment, and learning curves serve as an analytic tool for examining that return
